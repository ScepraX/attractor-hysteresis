# Attractor Hysteresis

> Entry into deep states is easy; exit is not — and not by the same route. The architecture where autism and ADHD co-occur. 

![Title Page](titlepage.png)

This document describes a specific cognitive architecture that arises when ASD (Autism Spectrum Disorder) and ADHD (Attention Deficit Hyperactivity Disorder) co-occur, and that behaves like neither condition alone. Its defining feature reduces to a single mechanism: engagement pulls the system into deep, stable states, but the way out is executive-gated, unstable, and not under deliberate control. Capture and release run on different processes, so the current state depends on the path that produced it, not only on present input. That asymmetry — taken precisely, not metaphorically, from the physics of hysteretic systems — is what the framework is built around.
The account runs on two levels held deliberately apart. The neural level is grounded in what resting-state brain-dynamics research has established; the experiential level in decades of sustained observation of the architecture's outputs. The two are mapped to each other only where the structural parallels justify it, and the mapping is treated as an interpretive frame, not a derivation. What follows sets out what makes this architecture distinct, why existing clinical models only partly fit it, and what follows from it across attention, emotion, memory, and capability.

*Derived from the intersection of the neural research of "Distinct Frontoparietal Brain Dynamics Underlying the Co-Occurrence of Autism and ADHD" (Watanabe & Watanabe, eNeuro, 2023; https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10348445/) and decades of structural and behavioural observation of the architecture's outputs. The neural description and the experiential description are one system seen from two positions, not two competing accounts. This is not a clinical document.*

*Tags: Autism, ADHD, Neurodiversity, Psychology, Neuroscience.*

## 1. The Core Dynamic

A system settles into stable states and resists leaving them. The full set of stable states, the unstable states between them, and the cost of moving between them is the **attractor landscape**. A stable state the system settles into and holds is an **attractor**, or a **basin**. The deeper the basin, the harder it is to leave.

A **hysteretic** system has asymmetric entry and exit thresholds: its current state depends on the path that brought it there, not only on present inputs. Entry and exit are different processes governed by different mechanisms. This is a physical property of certain nonlinear systems. The term is used here because it names the actual mechanism, not as metaphor.

**Attractor hysteresis** is the name for the combined ASD+ADHD architecture. Its structure is autism-like at the base: deep basins, strong resistance to transition. What distinguishes it is the exit mechanism. Exit runs through an executive gate that is itself unstable, and when that gate fires it crosses by a pathway that does not retrace the route of entry. Capture into a deep state happens one way; ejection out of it happens another. That asymmetry — the dependence of the current state on the history of transitions — is the hysteresis.

The remainder of this document describes the neural substrate of that dynamic, the topology it produces, the way inputs act on it, its full arc from engagement to collapse and recovery, and the consequences across emotion, memory, sleep, and capability.

## 2. The Neural Substrate

### 2.1 Energy Landscape Analysis

At any moment the brain occupies one of a small number of stable activity patterns across large-scale cortical networks. These patterns can be mapped as an energy landscape: valleys are stable states, ridges are the cost of moving between them. The system drifts toward valleys and resists crossing ridges. Comparing pure ASD, pure ADHD, ASD+ADHD, and neurotypical brains on this landscape produces distinct topologies for each — and the combined configuration is not a superposition of the two pure conditions. It is a third topology with its own structure.

### 2.2 Pure Overstable Architecture (ASD)

Stable states are unusually deep. The system settles hard and resists transition. Socio-communicative symptoms correlate with this overstability. Transitions occur less often than in neurotypical brains and require deliberate effort, but the transition mechanism itself is intact — costly, not absent or decoupled.

### 2.3 Pure Unstable Architecture (ADHD)

The landscape is flatter. Basins are shallow, unstable states are prominent, transitions are frequent. The driver is a short intrinsic neural timescale in a focal region — the left inferior parietal sulcus — which cannot hold its own state. That short timescale propagates to its parent network, the dorsal attention network, producing overly flexible whole-brain dynamics along diffuse, multi-hop pathways. The defining problem is not absent transitions but transitions that operate independently of intention.

### 2.4 Attractor Hysteresis Architecture (ASD+ADHD)

The landscape is a shallower version of the pure-ASD topology — still deeper and more stable than pure ADHD, still autism-like in basic structure, with a small number of states slightly deeper still. This is not a blend of the two conditions. It is a modified version of one parent architecture, with a transition mechanism present in neither.

The mechanism has a concrete neural form. A focal region in the **left prefrontal cortex** (the superior frontal gyrus) has an atypically short intrinsic neural timescale: it cannot hold state. That focal instability propagates to its parent network, the **frontoparietal control network** — the executive system that governs top-down regulation of cognitive state. A region that cannot hold state, sitting inside the network that regulates every other state, is the hysteresis at the neural level.

The causal chain runs: short-timescale focal region → reduced timescale of its parent network → an atypically frequent transition along one specific pathway through intermediate states. The pathway is specific and named (A–C–F–B); it is not the diffuse routing of pure ADHD.

The result is a **double dissociation**, and this is what establishes the architecture as a distinct third topology rather than an overlap. The comorbidity's specific pathway correlates with its cognitive instability but with no symptom of pure ADHD. Conversely, the pure-ADHD pathway does not account for the comorbidity's instability at all. The two instabilities are mechanically different routes with different neural origins.

Socio-communicative rigidity continues to be governed by the same overstable mechanism as pure ASD. The instability is the separate, new route. Capture into deep states is autism-like; ejection runs through the unstable frontoparietal gate along its non-retracing pathway. Entry and exit are structurally different processes — the hysteresis.

### 2.5 Evidence Base and Scope

The neural foundation is a single 2023 study comparing brain-state dynamics in children aged 5 to 13. Adult-specific research on this architecture is essentially absent — a substantive gap. The experiential account in this document extends the model into domains the study did not measure: sensory processing, interoception, emotion, memory, sleep. Those extensions are observations of system behaviour, and where they go beyond what the neural evidence covers they are marked as such. They are stated as observed mechanism requiring further investigation, not as claims derived from the available neural data.

## 3. The State Topology

Four landscapes, described on their own terms. These are models, not diagnostic categories or points on a single continuum.

### 3.1 Neurotypical Architecture

Moderate basin depth. The system settles but does not lock. Transition costs are low to moderate and largely automatic. Time flows with reasonable continuity. The self is largely consistent across contexts and does not require reconstruction after transitions. This marks a central tendency; variance within the architecture is significant.

### 3.2 Pure Overstable Architecture

Deep, narrow basins. Leaving a state requires deliberate effort; the transition mechanism is costly but intact. The present is densely saturated with detail — time as an extended, immersive now rather than a flow. Self-presence survives deep absorption: fully identified with the current focus but persisting as a coherent point through it. Transitions are effortful, but the self crosses them intact.

A direct consequence of overstability: core thinking patterns and ways of engaging do not shift substantially across the lifespan. The architecture that resists state transitions also resists the iterative self-revision that characterises neurotypical development. This is not arrested development; it is the same property operating at a longer timescale.

### 3.3 Pure Unstable Architecture

Shallow, transient basins. States form and dissolve easily, frequently without deliberate choice, driven by attentional-network instability. Time is vivid but fragmented. The self is fluid and reactive — continuous, but thinly so. Transitions are active and frequent; the problem is not their absence but their independence from intention.

### 3.4 Attractor Hysteresis Architecture

Deep basins, autism-like in basic structure. The gate governing exit is an executive system that is itself unstable: when it fires it follows a pathway that does not trace back the route of entry. Entry and exit are structurally different processes.

Time is punctuated. Long periods of deep, timeless engagement, followed by abrupt positional discontinuities. Not continuous flow, not staccato fragmentation. Crystallised, then broken.

The self is discontinuous across closing transitions. Deep absorption produces total engagement, the same as in the pure overstable architecture. What is specific to this architecture is what happens at the transition. When the basin closes, the prior state becomes inaccessible, and what forms afterward is assembled from residue rather than retrieved intact. The continuity across the crossing is not preserved.

The functional consequence is an input-processing gap during the crossing: environmental information arriving in that window is not received. This is invisible from inside the system and conspicuous from outside it, producing a systematic divergence between the internal and external accounts of the same event.

Core cognitive style persists across the lifespan, as in the pure overstable architecture — same property, same mechanism.

## 4. The Input Layer

### 4.1 No Membrane

Every external channel — scent, touch, sound, taste — enters unfiltered. None is ever background. Input that matches the current state stabilises it; input that mismatches it disrupts directly. Any channel can break the current basin or force a closed one back open.

Because the effect of an input depends on the current state, the same input stabilises in one state and is aversive in another. This is not inconsistency. The state changed; the input did not. A practical corollary: the moment a previously stabilising input becomes disruptive is a reliable real-time signal that a state transition has occurred or is in progress. It is information about the system's state, not dysregulation.

Structure, complexity, rhythm, register, and resonance with the current state all determine whether a given input stabilises or destabilises. What works is not fixed; it is state-dependent.

### 4.2 Hyperfocus as the Only Block

Deep engagement is the only state that blocks input. The block is lossy: strain accumulates beneath it and discharges on drop-out, as both somatic load and emotional state. Stress, burnout, and exhaustion add further sensitivity on top of this baseline — they are an additional load, not the baseline itself. The unfiltered condition is the default; the block is the exception, and it is not free.

### 4.3 Interoception

Internally generated signals — hunger, thirst, fatigue — act below the executive layer as basin-destabilisers rather than as felt signals that get noticed and managed. Outside deep engagement they reliably lower the current state and tend to cause drop-out, which is why they function as a natural transition trigger. A sufficiently deep basin blocks even these; they then surface as accumulated strain on ejection.

Because these signals are not reliably felt-and-handled, externally scheduling them — enforced eating, drinking, sleeping — is the functional substitute for interoceptive regulation. The standard description of being "out of touch with the body" is accurate as far as it goes, but the mechanism is specific: the signals are present and act on the system; they simply do not arrive as managed conscious information.

### 4.4 Below-Layer Modulators

Some inputs reliably modulate basins below the executive layer, bypassing the unstable regulatory mechanism entirely. They share one property: they shift the state because the input environment shifted, not because regulation was applied.

**Structured auditory input (music)** is the most versatile. It functions as a basin stabiliser during deep engagement, a transition bridge between states, a capture mechanism during recovery, and an exit route from a blocking emotional state — all without invoking deliberate regulation.

**Physical movement** has a reliable positive effect that is largely unconditional, not dependent on current state or available capacity in the way most interventions are. Combined with structured auditory input it is more than additive — the pair can produce transitions across larger cognitive distances than either alone.

**Substances** modulate basins strongly and consistently. The response to a given class is predictable rather than variable: agents that increase arousal deepen capture and sharpen lockout; agents that flatten arousal flatten the landscape toward forced, uncontrolled transitions. The effect is a reliable feature of the topology rather than the typically reported general effect.

**Temperature** has a large and consistent impact on state.

Deep pressure and specific light conditions are strongly suspected to operate the same way but are not confirmed here, and are listed as open in section 11.

## 5. The Dynamics: The Full Arc

The sequence from deep engagement to collapse and recovery. Not every instance reaches collapse, but the full arc defines the architecture's boundary conditions and failure modes.

### 5.1 Entry and Deep Engagement

A sufficiently compelling stimulus captures the system and the basin deepens. Engagement becomes total — not a matter of degree but a qualitative state in which self-presence is absent and the system is fully merged with the object of engagement.

A specific consequence of sustained deep engagement is **basin mirroring**: the content of the engaged material temporarily restructures aspects of the active architecture itself — language, register, perspective, conceptual framing. This is not deliberate imitation. After deep engagement with a body of content, that content's vocabulary, tone, and internal logic become operationally present in subsequent cognitive activity and persist across multiple later transitions, potentially for days. The prior basin does not merely leave traces; it temporarily modifies the active architecture.

### 5.2 Transition Types

Three distinct transitions, which must be kept apart.

**Interruption without closure.** A signal disrupts the current engagement but does not close the basin. The attractor remains open; return is possible. Each interruption carries a cost, and accumulating interruptions degrade the depth of subsequent re-entry until a basin that has never formally closed becomes progressively harder to enter deeply. Outside hyperfocus, where there is no membrane, interruption is close to constant; inside hyperfocus input is blocked, but the strain accumulates underneath (section 4.2).

**Ejection with closure.** The executive gate fires fully. The system crosses its non-retracing pathway and arrives in a different state, and the prior basin closes completely — the content that held total focus becomes immediately and completely inaccessible. Not difficult to re-enter: inaccessible. This is qualitatively distinct from the pure unstable architecture's diffuse loss of focus. Focus does not drift; the basin locks shut. Full ejection is less frequent than interruption but produces the lockout in section 5.4.

**Partial transition.** The system begins crossing toward a new state but does not complete the crossing. The prior basin is no longer fully accessible; the new basin has not stabilised; the system occupies an intermediate position. Large-distance transitions — deep technical absorption to social interaction, for instance — frequently produce this. Multiple attempts may be needed before the new state stabilises, and rapid large-distance partials deplete available capacity quickly, because each carries the executive cost without delivering the stability of a completed crossing.

### 5.3 The Interference Mechanism

A general property underlying much of the arc: deliberate conscious attention directed at any automatic process degrades that process. When the process runs on its own it functions. When deliberate control is applied to it, it breaks down.

The mechanism is identifiable and matches the neural account. Deliberate control is top-down regulation — the frontoparietal control network attempting to govern a target process. In a stable architecture this produces clean modulation. In this architecture the frontoparietal control network is itself unstable (the short prefrontal timescale of section 2.4), so top-down activation injects noise at the interface between executive control and the target instead of regulating it. The automatic process receives a dysregulated signal from above rather than being left to run. The more deliberate the attempt, the stronger the unstable network's activation, and the greater the interference.

This is not a general failure of executive function. The instability is specific to the frontoparietal control network's top-down regulatory role; processes that run below its intervention range are unaffected — which is precisely why the below-layer inputs of section 4.4 work. They engage the system beneath the executive layer and bypass the unstable mechanism entirely.

The consequences are wide:

- Directing attention to breathing or bodily processes applies top-down regulation to systems that normally run autonomously. The unstable network injects interference into autonomic processes, producing dysregulation. This is the neural basis of the panic response to body-focused attention practices.
- Chronic stress that cannot resolve into a stable basin produces sustained low-level frontoparietal activation as the system repeatedly attempts and fails to regulate. The accumulated interference routes into the body as somatic symptoms — the physical expression of a regulatory system that is continuously active and continuously failing to produce stable output.
- Forced re-entry into a closed basin activates the unstable network directly onto the target, producing the interference that prevents automatic engagement from occurring (section 5.4).

### 5.4 Post-Ejection Lockout and the Cascade

After full ejection the closed basin cannot be re-entered by deliberate effort. The attempt to force re-entry activates the unstable frontoparietal network top-down onto the target, and the deliberate effort is precisely what prevents the automatic process of basin entry from functioning. Each forced attempt activates the unstable network again, compounds the interference, and worsens the state.

The arc is not linear degradation but an accelerating cascade: repeated attempts destabilise not only the target basin but the system's capacity to settle into any stable state. Protective avoidance of forced re-entry is therefore the rational response to a real cascade risk, not a behavioural symptom. Frameworks that treat the avoidance as the pathology have the causal direction inverted.

### 5.5 Systemic Collapse

If the cascade is not interrupted, the result is systemic collapse: loss of the capacity to settle into any stable state. The system becomes stateless.

Statelessness is itself the primary stress — not the loss of any particular content, but the absence of any stable ground. This stress is structurally difficult to identify or communicate because it has no object. There is nothing lost that can be pointed to. There is only the absence of the stable states that normally structure experience and enable function. Sustained statelessness routes into the body as somatic symptoms.

### 5.6 Recovery

Recovery from collapse cannot be self-initiated. Deliberate effort to recover invokes the interference of section 5.3 and compounds the state. Recovery occurs when an external or environmental stimulus is compelling enough to open a new basin and capture the system. The prior state does not resolve; it ends because something else begins.

## 6. Emotion as State

Emotion in this architecture is a full-system state — a basin — not a gradable feeling layered over a stable self and turned up or down at will. It is entered and exited the same way every other state is: by capture, not by regulation. Direct management of an emotional state is a no-op. The only exit is capture into a different state, which takes time and engagement directed elsewhere, exactly as recovery does in section 5.6.

There is no reliable introspective access to the state. The emotional state is generally not available to awareness as it occurs. It is knowable mainly afterward, through its effects — a dropped basin, somatic load, a change in behaviour, an external attribution — and even that reading is effortful and unreliable, because the self-analysis it requires runs through the same unstable executive that struggles to hold any state on its own.

This produces a three-layer divergence: the state itself, the internal read of the state, and the external attribution of the state can each differ from the others. External reports ("you're angry") frequently match no internal read at all. Training built on the assumption of introspective access does not resolve this, because the access it assumes is not present — the same structural reason such training tends to underperform here.

Because no channel is buffered (section 4.1), input drives emotional state directly. Mismatched input can force a transition straight into a disruptive emotional state, immediately and without intermediate build-up. In childhood this could surface behaviourally as aggression. The mechanism is the same forced transition seen elsewhere, on the emotional axis: an input crossed a threshold and the state changed. And the standard observation that "deliberately trying to relax intensifies it" is the forced-re-entry failure of section 5.4 applied to emotion — top-down regulation applied to a state that only environmental capture can change.

## 7. Memory

Memory in this architecture is indexed by basin, not by time. Content encoded inside a deep basin is available on re-entry into that basin: re-entering floods it back nearly complete, and re-experiencing the original input — rereading, rewatching — reinstates the state with its original fidelity, often in advance of the content itself. Outside the basin, with no associative route in, decontextualised recall largely fails.

Recall is re-entry, not retrieval on demand. This is why context-free testing performs poorly while deep understanding remains fully intact: the understanding is not gone, it is basin-bound. The capability of section 10 — that deep understanding compounds without erosion — holds precisely because the content is available on re-entry, not because it is available at will. The two are different claims, and only the first is true for this architecture.

## 8. Sleep Boundary Transitions

The transitions into and out of sleep are subject to the same architectural properties as daytime transitions, with specific and testable consequences.

**Sleep onset** is one of the largest transitions the system makes. Large-distance transitions cannot be forced, and forcing one activates the frontoparietal control network top-down onto a process that must run automatically to function. Standard sleep-hygiene recommendations that require deliberate management of the transition — structured relaxation, controlled breathing, body scanning — invoke exactly the mechanism that fails. The compatible approach is the same as daytime recovery: environmental capture by passive below-layer inputs that do not demand deliberate attention.

**Waking** is abrupt, not gradual. The system crosses from sleep to full waking state immediately, with no transitional buffer — the same threshold-crossing property as daytime transitions. This is independently observable and is one of the framework's more directly testable predictions.

**Continuity across the boundary** follows from whether the basin closes. Daytime ejection closes the basin: the prior state becomes inaccessible, a new state assembles from residue, and the self is discontinuous across the crossing (section 3.4). Sleep does not close the pre-sleep basin — nothing ejected the system, the basin simply stayed open — so the same basin is re-entered on waking and the self is continuous across the crossing. Same residue mechanism, opposite outcome, decided entirely by whether the transition closed the basin. This is why the state held before sleep still feels active on waking.

**Dream inaccessibility** follows from the abrupt waking crossing. Encoding dream content into accessible memory appears to require a traversable boundary — a gradual crossing that can carry content across. The abrupt waking transition closes that window before encoding can occur: the dreams existed, but the crossing was too fast to carry them through. The exception confirms the rule: on the rare occasion when waking is gradual rather than abrupt, the boundary becomes traversable in both directions, producing the lucid or shapeable state otherwise absent. Its rarity is consistent with gradual transitions not being the default mode. **Nightmares** survive because the intensity of the content generates the ejection itself; the content arrives in waking memory as the cause of the crossing rather than being lost in it. External forced waking — sound, light, physical sensation — does the same: an external capture causes the crossing, and dream content present at that moment can survive into waking memory.

## 9. Working With the Architecture

Standard intervention frameworks assume deliberate conscious control is the primary lever for managing cognitive and emotional states. For this architecture that assumption is structurally incorrect. What functions instead is environmental: the state changes because the input environment changes, not because effort is applied to the state directly.

The general principle: **select the environment, allow capture to occur.** The deliberate act sits one level up from state management — choosing inputs likely to produce the needed capture. The state follows from the environment; it cannot be forced directly.

**What is compatible** are the below-layer inputs of section 4.4 — structured auditory input, movement, temperature, and the substance and below-layer modulators — because they act beneath the unstable executive layer. Self-paced input is strongly favoured over externally-paced: self-paced text lets a basin deepen on its own schedule, while externally-paced media such as video or live speech impose transition timing the architecture cannot control and force partial transitions. Externally-paced media remain usable for passive entertainment but not for deep engagement.

**Task initiation** is binary capture, not a coercible decision. A necessary but uncompelling task either captures the system — in which case it begins immediately — or does not register, in which case it is gone. The second case is not avoidance; it is absence. External pressure — deadlines, urgency, being shouted at — does not raise an uncompelling task past the capture threshold. "Just start" names a lever the architecture does not have. What changes initiation is the same as what changes everything else: whether the input crosses the capture threshold.

**What is excluded**, by the same logic, are interventions that require deliberate conscious attention directed at internal states:

- Mindfulness and present-moment awareness directed at bodily or mental processes apply the unstable executive to automatic systems, producing interference rather than regulation.
- Cognitive reframing applies top-down force to the current state — the same mechanism that breaks automatic processes.
- Forced re-engagement with closed basins follows the cascade of section 5.4.

The failure of these approaches here is not treatment resistance. It is the predictable result of applying the wrong intervention logic to a specific system.

## 10. Capabilities

The same architectural properties that produce the costs above also produce specific capabilities with no direct equivalent in other architectures.

**Total engagement** produces output quality that distributed attention cannot replicate. For problems where depth of thinking is the differentiator, the capacity to fully inhabit a problem — with self-presence absent rather than merely reduced — produces qualitatively different results.

**Basin mirroring** enables genuine perspective integration. Deep engagement temporarily restructures the active architecture, producing not just knowledge about a domain but temporary operational fluency within its internal logic, beyond what deliberate study yields.

**Cross-domain structural recognition** follows from systems-level thinking combined with deep basin access. At sufficient depth the underlying structure of a system becomes visible, and that structure recurs across domains that appear unrelated at the surface. What looks like analogy from outside is, from inside, recognition of structural identity.

**Cognitive stability across the lifespan** means deep understanding compounds without erosion and remains available on re-entry (section 7). This is directly valuable for long-horizon problems where accumulated understanding is the primary asset.

**Positioning at the edge of the known** is a mechanism, not a chosen orientation. Once a domain is fully understood, its problems no longer generate sufficient basin depth to sustain capture, so capture migrates toward the unsolved. The architecture does not decide to seek novelty; solved problems passively lose the capacity to capture it. The functional result is that engagement collects at the boundary of what is solved.

## 11. Open Questions

This framework is a foundation, not a conclusion. The following are opened by it rather than answered by it:

- The precise relationship between neural basin depth and the disappearance of self-presence in deep engagement.
- The threshold between partial absorption and the complete absence of self-presence within a single architecture.
- Whether absorption suppresses acute pain (absorption as analgesia). This remains indeterminate, because the deep-basin state and the pain-receiving state almost never co-occur — depth tends to preclude the actions that produce acute pain — so the two cannot be cleanly compared from observation so far. Attention amplifying pain is established (section 9 logic); the analgesia question is open.
- Whether deep pressure and specific light conditions function as reliable below-layer modulators in the way temperature and structured auditory input do.
- Whether repeated deep engagement with a domain produces permanent architectural modification or only temporary restructuring (the durability of basin mirroring).
- The full topology of capture-compatible inputs beyond those identified here, and whether structural properties predict compatibility.
- How this architecture interacts with conditions beyond ASD and ADHD. The framework is explicitly scoped to the two; the broader landscape is unmapped.
- The adult-evidence gap: the neural foundation is drawn from children aged 5 to 13, and adult-specific data is essentially absent.

## 12. Index of Findable Questions

The questions below are phrased the way the environment trains them to be phrased — pointed inward, observer-shaped, in the form they take when asked of oneself, typed into a search field, or brought to a clinician. The phrasing is kept deliberately in that form so the description can be located by it. Each is answered as a statement about the system.

**Focus and attention**
- *Why can focus run intensely for hours and then stop completely, with no way back in?* — Full ejection closes the basin; a closed basin is inaccessible, not merely hard to re-enter (5.2, 5.4).
- *Why does forcing a refocus make it worse?* — Forced re-entry activates the unstable executive onto the target, compounding interference in an accelerating cascade (5.3, 5.4).
- *Why does focus fail on things there is real skill and interest in?* — Capture depends on the input crossing the threshold in the current state, not on competence or desire (4.1, 9).
- *Why does the ability to focus seem unrelated to wanting to?* — Initiation is capture, not decision; willing it is not the lever (9).

**Transitions and switching**
- *Why are abrupt changes so costly even without emotional investment in the prior task?* — Transitions carry an executive cost regardless of content; large-distance crossings produce depleting partial transitions (5.2).
- *Why does switching between very different tasks deplete so much faster?* — Each large-distance partial transition carries the executive cost without delivering a completed, stable state (5.2).
- *Why are multiple attempts sometimes needed to engage with something new?* — Partial transitions leave the system between basins until one stabilises (5.2).

**Interventions that do not work**
- *Why does mindfulness make things worse?* — Body- or mind-focused attention applies the unstable executive to automatic systems, producing interference, not regulation (5.3, 9).
- *Why does deliberately trying to relax often intensify the state?* — Direct regulation of a state is forced re-entry on the emotional axis; only environmental capture changes it (5.4, 6).
- *Why do breathing exercises sometimes produce panic?* — Top-down attention to an autonomic process injects interference into it (5.3).
- *Why do standard ASD or ADHD strategies fail to deliver?* — They assume a stable executive and a single parent condition; this architecture is a third topology with an unstable gate (2.4, 9).

**Sensory input**
- *Why is every sound, smell, touch, or taste so intense, never in the background?* — No channel is buffered; all input enters unfiltered (4.1).
- *Why does the same input help in one moment and become unbearable the next?* — The effect is state-dependent; the state changed, the input did not, and the switch is itself a transition signal (4.1).
- *Why is hyperfocus the only thing that blocks it, and why is there a price afterward?* — Deep engagement is the only block, and it is lossy: strain accumulates and discharges on drop-out (4.2).

**Body and interoception**
- *Why does the body seem disconnected, with hunger or fatigue noticed only late?* — Interoceptive signals act below the executive as basin-destabilisers rather than as managed conscious signals (4.3).
- *Why does sustained stress produce physical symptoms rather than primarily psychological ones?* — Continuous failed regulation routes accumulated interference into the body (5.3, 5.5).
- *Why does paying attention to pain or an itch make it worse?* — Top-down attention to the process amplifies rather than regulates it (5.3, 9).

**Emotion**
- *Why is there a mismatch between what others say is felt and what is actually experienced?* — The state, the internal read, and the external attribution diverge; introspective access to the state is not reliably present (6).
- *Why does managing emotions feel like a no-op?* — Emotion is a full-system state exited only by capture into a different state, not by regulation (6).
- *Why can mismatched input tip straight into a disruptive state with no build-up?* — Unfiltered input forces a transition directly into an emotional basin (4.1, 6).

**Memory and testing**
- *Why does rereading or rewatching bring everything back complete, while cold recall fails?* — Memory is basin-indexed; recall is re-entry, not retrieval on demand (7).
- *Why is decontextualised testing so much harder than the actual understanding would predict?* — The understanding is intact but basin-bound; a context-free test removes the route in (7).

**Sleep**
- *Why does waking happen immediately and fully, with no transition period?* — Waking is an abrupt threshold crossing with no buffer (8).
- *Why are dreams rarely remembered?* — Encoding needs a traversable boundary; the abrupt crossing closes the window before encoding (8).
- *Why does trying to fall asleep deliberately make it harder?* — Sleep onset is a large-distance transition that cannot be forced; forcing it invokes interference (8).
- *Why does the state held before sleep still feel active on waking?* — Sleep does not close the pre-sleep basin, so the same basin is re-entered on waking (8).

**Identity and continuity**
- *Why does intense engagement with a book, project, or conversation leave a feeling of being a different person afterward?* — Basin mirroring temporarily restructures the active architecture (5.1).
- *Why does the language or perspective of something engaged deeply persist for days?* — The prior basin modifies the active architecture and the modification outlasts the crossing (5.1).
- *Why does the core thinking style feel unchanged since childhood?* — Overstability resists self-revision; core style persists across the lifespan (3.2, 3.4).

**Substances and temperature**
- *Why do substances change the ability to engage so strongly and predictably?* — They modulate basin depth and stability below the executive layer, with a consistent per-class effect (4.4).
- *Why does temperature shift the state so much?* — Temperature is a reliable below-layer modulator (4.4).

**Professional and clinical experience**
- *Why do ASD frameworks only partially fit?* — The socio-communicative rigidity is shared with pure ASD, but the instability is a separate mechanism ASD frameworks do not model (2.4).
- *Why do ADHD frameworks only partially fit?* — The instability has a different neural origin and a different pathway than pure ADHD; it is a double dissociation (2.4).
- *Why have specialists in either condition been unable to place the full picture?* — The architecture is a distinct third topology, not a sum of the two they specialise in (2.1, 2.4).
- *Why did burnout-recovery protocols make things significantly worse?* — They assume deliberate regulation and forced rest; both invoke the interference and cascade mechanisms (5.3, 5.4, 9).
